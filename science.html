The basics

Your body runs on a 24-hour clock.

Your circadian rhythm is a biological timer embedded in nearly every cell of your body. It regulates sleep, wakefulness, hormone release, digestion, and dozens of other functions on a roughly 24-hour cycle.

When you fly across time zones, the external clock (daylight and social cues) shifts immediately, but your internal clock takes days to follow. That gap is jet lag.

The good news: your circadian clock is trainable. Specific inputs — light, darkness, caffeine, melatonin, sleep timing — can shift it faster than just waiting. Sandpiper calculates exactly which input to apply and when.

Seek Light

Your most powerful circadian signal.

The science

Light is the primary zeitgeber (German for "time-giver") for the human circadian clock. Specialised photoreceptive cells in your retina — intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs) — detect light independently of vision and relay the signal directly to the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) in the hypothalamus, your master clock.

Morning light suppresses melatonin and advances your clock (makes it run earlier). The effect is strongest in the first few hours after your body's internal dawn, and it's dose-dependent — outdoor light on a cloudy day (10,000–25,000 lux) is still 100× brighter than most indoor lighting.

What Sandpiper uses it for

When you're flying east and need to advance your clock, Sandpiper schedules seek-light events in your destination's morning window. When flying west, it may shift light exposure to later in the day to delay the clock instead. The timing is calculated from your specific departure and arrival times.

Go outside — even overcast skies provide far more lux than indoor lighting.
Aim for 20–30 minutes of exposure. Face toward the sky, not directly at the sun.
A 10,000-lux light therapy lamp works as a substitute on the plane or in a windowless hotel room.
Avoid Light

Light at the wrong time sets you back.

The science

Light exposure has opposite effects depending on when it hits your circadian phase. Light in the evening (relative to your body clock) delays the clock — pushing your sleep later. If you're trying to advance your clock to an eastern destination, evening light is actively counterproductive.

Blue-wavelength light (peaking around 480 nm) is the most potent circadian signal. Screens, LED overhead lights, and fluorescent office lighting are heavy in blue wavelengths. Even moderate exposure (50–200 lux) in the biological evening suppresses melatonin for hours.

What Sandpiper uses it for

Avoid-light cards protect your clock during the hours when light would push it in the wrong direction. This is especially important in the evening before your destination's local bedtime, and immediately after an eastbound flight when your body still thinks it's daytime back home.

Enable night mode / warm-color shift on all screens at least 2 hours before destination bedtime.
Use dim, amber-toned lamps instead of overhead lights.
On the plane during a sleep window, close the window shade and wear an eye mask.
Wake

A fixed wake time anchors everything else.

The science

Wake time is one of the most reliable anchors for the circadian rhythm. Waking at a consistent local time reinforces the adenosine build-up/clearance cycle (sleep pressure) and aligns the morning cortisol surge — the "cortisol awakening response" — to the correct local dawn.

Research on shift workers and transcontinental travelers consistently shows that irregular wake times prolong circadian misalignment, while a strict wake anchor accelerates re-entrainment even when sleep feels poor.

What Sandpiper uses it for

Wake events mark the target wake time in destination local time. Even if you slept poorly, waking at this time is critical — it sets the starting point for the light exposure, caffeine window, and melatonin timing that follow throughout the day.

Set a hard alarm. Do not snooze — each snooze cycle adds adenosine confusion.
Get up and move immediately. Lying in bed delays the cortisol surge that helps you feel alert.
Pair wake time with immediate light exposure for maximum effect.
Stay Awake

Resist the urge to sleep at the wrong time.

The science

Sleep is regulated by two processes: circadian rhythm (Process C) and homeostatic sleep pressure (Process S, driven by adenosine accumulation). Sleeping during destination daytime satisfies Process S but reinforces your old Process C — meaning you'll feel better short-term but prolong jet lag for days.

Staying awake through fatigue, even when it's uncomfortable, forces adenosine to keep building until it aligns with destination nighttime. This is often the hardest part of the schedule, but also one of the highest-leverage interventions.

What Sandpiper uses it for

Stay-awake cards appear during destination daytime hours when your body clock still expects sleep. They're paired with caffeine and light events to make the effort manageable. The goal is to keep you functional until a destination-aligned bedtime.

Move — a 10-minute walk does more for alertness than any amount of sitting.
A nap of 10–20 minutes is acceptable during stay-awake periods if necessary. Longer naps restart a full sleep cycle and make it harder to sleep at night.
Social interaction is a powerful alertness driver — meet people, make calls, avoid isolation.
Caffeine

A strategic alertness tool, not just a habit.

The science

Caffeine works by binding adenosine receptors in the brain without activating them, effectively blocking the sleep-pressure signal for 4–6 hours. It doesn't eliminate sleep need — it delays it. When caffeine clears, adenosine floods back in rapidly, which is why mistimed caffeine leads to a hard crash.

Caffeine also has a half-life of approximately 5–7 hours in most adults (longer in some). A 200mg dose (two espressos) consumed at 2pm still has 100mg active at 9pm — enough to measurably delay sleep onset and reduce slow-wave sleep quality, even if you feel like you can fall asleep.

What Sandpiper uses it for

Caffeine events are placed during destination daytime hours to support wakefulness when your body clock is pushing for sleep. Sandpiper calculates both the open window (when caffeine helps) and the cutoff (before which all caffeine should be finished).

100–200mg per dose (1–2 cups of coffee). More is not more effective and increases anxiety.
Don't drink caffeine on an empty stomach — it amplifies the jitteriness and accelerates metabolism, shortening the alertness window.
Always respect Sandpiper's caffeine cutoff, even if you feel fine.
No Caffeine

The cutoff matters more than the coffee.

The science

Because caffeine blocks adenosine receptors, caffeine taken too late prevents the natural rise of sleep pressure needed to fall asleep at the destination-aligned bedtime. It also suppresses the secretion of melatonin — the darkness hormone — by blocking adenosine pathways that normally trigger the pineal gland to release it.

Critically, caffeine's effects on sleep architecture continue well after subjective alertness fades. Even if you feel tired and can fall asleep, residual caffeine reduces slow-wave (deep) sleep — the most restorative phase — by up to 20%, leaving you less recovered despite the same number of hours in bed.

What Sandpiper uses it for

No-caffeine cards mark the point at which further caffeine would compromise destination-aligned sleep. Sandpiper calculates the cutoff by working backward from your target bedtime, accounting for caffeine half-life. For most people this is 6–8 hours before bedtime.

Switch to herbal tea, water, or decaffeinated drinks after the cutoff. Decaf still contains 5–30mg of caffeine — check labels on long haul flights.
Be aware that dark chocolate and some medications (certain pain relievers) contain caffeine.
If you feel a crash shortly after stopping caffeine, a short walk and cold water are more effective than another cup.
Melatonin

Darkness in a pill — timed precisely.

The science

Melatonin is produced by the pineal gland in response to darkness and acts as a circadian signal — telling the brain that night has begun. Unlike a sleeping pill, melatonin doesn't sedate you directly; it shifts your body clock. Its effectiveness is almost entirely determined by when you take it, not how much.

Taking melatonin in the early evening (relative to the destination clock) advances the circadian phase — shifting the clock earlier, ideal after eastbound flights. Taking it in the early morning (at home time) has less value and can cause grogginess. Research shows doses as low as 0.5mg can be as effective as 5mg for circadian shifting, while larger doses primarily extend duration of drowsiness.

What Sandpiper uses it for

Melatonin events are placed at the destination's early evening to help advance the clock on eastbound routes. Sandpiper calculates the optimal window based on the phase response curve (PRC) — the relationship between melatonin timing and clock shift magnitude.

Use 0.5mg–1mg for circadian shifting. Reserve higher doses (3–5mg) only when you need the sedating effect to fall asleep in an unfamiliar environment.
Take it 30–60 minutes before the target time shown in the app.
Pair with avoid-light behavior after taking melatonin — light exposure immediately counteracts its effect.
Consult a healthcare provider if you take medications — melatonin interacts with anticoagulants, immunosuppressants, and some diabetes drugs.
Sleep

Sleep at the right local time, not just when you're tired.

The science

Sleep is the primary restorative phase for the circadian system. During sleep — particularly slow-wave (deep) sleep — the brain clears metabolic waste, consolidates memory, and performs cellular repair. For jet lag recovery, the timing of sleep matters as much as its duration.

Sleeping during destination nighttime reinforces the new circadian phase. Each destination-aligned sleep episode shifts Process C a little further toward the local time. Conversely, sleeping during destination daytime keeps the old phase alive and extends jet lag duration significantly.

What Sandpiper uses it for

Sleep events mark planned windows that align with destination nighttime — both on the plane and in your hotel. On long-haul flights, Sandpiper identifies which portion of the flight overlaps with destination night and marks that as the sleep window, even if it's midday back home.

Make the environment as sleep-conducive as possible: eye mask, earplugs or white noise, cool temperature (16–19°C / 61–66°F is optimal).
Don't watch the clock — checking the time activates the arousal system and makes sleep onset take longer.
If you can't sleep, lie still with eyes closed. Even quiet wakefulness in the dark is more restorative for circadian alignment than getting up and turning on lights.
Flight

The transition point that restructures your schedule.

The science

The flight itself isn't just travel — it's a circadian-disruption event. Cabin pressure is maintained at the equivalent of 6,000–8,000 feet altitude, reducing blood oxygen saturation by 4–6% compared to sea level. Combined with low humidity (~10–20%), reduced physical activity, and irregular meal timing, the flight environment actively worsens circadian disruption independently of the time zone change.

The direction of travel matters significantly. Eastbound flights require the clock to advance (get earlier), which is harder for the circadian system than delaying (westbound). Most people's free-running circadian period is slightly longer than 24 hours, making delay easier than advance.

What Sandpiper uses it for

Flight events (FLIGHT_DEPART and FLIGHT_ARRIVE) are reference points that divide your schedule into three phases — pre-flight, in-flight, and post-arrival. They also carry route information: IATA codes, departure times, and local time zone offsets that drive all the downstream calculations.

Set your watch to destination time at boarding. This mental switch primes you to interpret Sandpiper's schedule in local time.
Hydrate aggressively — dehydration at altitude impairs cognitive function and worsens fatigue.
Avoid alcohol in-flight. It disrupts sleep architecture (suppresses REM) even in small amounts and amplifies dehydration.
Move every hour — standing and walking counters blood pooling, reduces fatigue, and lowers deep-vein thrombosis risk.
